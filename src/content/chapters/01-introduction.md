Episodic memory is the cognitive system that allows us to mentally travel back in time, re-experiencing events from our personal past. Unlike semantic memory, which stores general facts and knowledge, episodic memory is inherently tied to a specific time and place — it is memory with a sense of self.

The medial temporal lobe, particularly the hippocampus, plays a central role in the encoding and retrieval of episodic memories. Damage to this region famously results in anterograde amnesia, as demonstrated by the well-known case of patient H.M., who could no longer form new long-term memories after bilateral hippocampal resection.

More recent research has revealed that episodic memory is not a monolithic process. Encoding, consolidation, and retrieval each engage distinct neural circuits, and the fidelity of a memory depends on the interplay between these stages. This chapter lays the theoretical groundwork for understanding how the brain constructs and reconstructs memories from continuous experience.

We will explore the key models of episodic memory — from Tulving's original formulation to modern computational accounts — and consider how these frameworks inform the experimental design of this project.
